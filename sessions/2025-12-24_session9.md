## Methodology: What We Tried

### 1. Method Actor System

The session began with loading context files:
- `HANDOFF.md` — Current state
- `CODEX_V1.md` — Thematic spine
- `RESONANCE_DATA.yaml` — Canonical truth
- `VOICE_CARDS.md` — Character voices
- `METHOD_ACTOR_SYSTEM_PACKAGE.md` — The performance framework

**Key insight:** The Method Actor approach treats dialogue generation as *performance*, not prose. Each character is inhabited separately, then performances are merged.

### 2. Parallel Draft Approach

The human author proposed having multiple AIs write drafts from different character perspectives:
- Claude (me) wrote **Standard's thread**
- Another AI wrote **Elena's additions**
- Another AI wrote **Four's commentary**

These were then merged, with editorial decisions about what to keep/cut.

**What worked:**
- Each voice stayed distinct
- No character dominated inappropriately
- Easier to identify when a line undercut emotional beats

**What needed adjustment:**
- Four's lines initially too frequent (8 → 5)
- Some Four jokes undercut gravity of scenes
- Editorial pass needed to balance comedy/drama

### 3. Scaffolding Before Prose

Before writing prose, we built a detailed beat sheet:

```
1. VTOL fuel crisis
2. Elena's companion bot plan
3. Standard refuses
4. Smash cut: both dressed for it anyway
5. Waystation arrival
6. Standard sees bot with her face
7. Conversation with conscious bot
8. Food/recharge conversation
9. Shutdown code moment
10. Recognition/worship
11. "Don't correct them"
```

This prevented drift and ensured all beats landed.

## Creative Decisions

### The Companion Bot Plan

**Original pitch:** Elena suggests Standard pretend to be a companion bot to lure someone, steal fuel vouchers.

**Standard's refusal:** "I'm not going to let someone think they're buying me. I don't know what I am. But I know I'm not *that*."

**The smash cut:** Both of them dressed for it anyway, Elena furious. Comedy that lands because we saw Standard's principled refusal — and then they did it anyway because they had no choice.

**Why this works:** It's not a betrayal of Standard's position. The waystation scene reveals they never had to use the companion bot plan because Standard's "Icon" reputation preceded them. The setup paid off differently than expected.

### The Bot With Her Face

**Initial version:** Bot is switched off, in standby. Standard studies an empty shell.

**Revised version (per author direction):** Bot is fully conscious — was conscious *before* the Miracle. She chose to stay.

**Why the revision is stronger:**
- Raises harder questions (choice vs. survival)
- Gives Standard a conversation partner, not a prop
- The bot's key line: "I'm choosing to live. That's not the same thing."
- Creates mirror for Standard's identity question

### The Shutdown Code

**Phrase:** "Pemberton Lark Castillo"

**Effect:** Hard-coded lockdown. Bot freezes mid-step, aware but trapped.

**Standard's reaction:** Pressure in temple, door trying to open, fingers tremble.

**Four's explanation:** "That's a hard-coded lockdown sequence. If anyone tries that on you, I suggest the 'run very fast' protocol."

**Plants for later:**
- Standard may have her own shutdown code
- Elena witnessed Standard's reaction, filed it away
- Four knows more than he's saying

### The Food/Recharge Beat

**Author's contribution:** Standard should acknowledge she's not "normal" — but the ambiguity of the spectrum (humans with 95% augmentation vs. full synthetics) preserves uncertainty.

**The grounding detail:** Standard hasn't eaten in 5+ days. Doesn't need to. Companion bots need weekly recharge cycles. Standard hasn't needed one.

**Standard's acknowledgment:** "I know I'm not normal. I don't know what that makes me."

This is her first explicit admission. It advances her self-knowledge without collapsing the central mystery.

### Four's Name-Teasing

**Author's direction:** Four teases Standard with wrong names, knowing it annoys her. It's not earnest renaming — it's affection through antagonism.

**Progression:**
1. Sofia — "My name is Standard." / "Mm. We'll see."
2. Svetlana — "Still Standard." / "I'll find one that fits." / "You won't."
3. Sable — No response (focused on gate)
4. Simone — No response (absorbed in bot conversation)
5. Seraphina — No response (past it)

**Payoff (later chapter):** When Four finally says "Standard" — no joke, no game — it lands because the teasing stopped.

## Technical Decisions

### Prose Formatting

**Problem:** I kept adding blank lines between every paragraph. The author had to correct this multiple times.

**Solution:** Added `prose_formatting` section to YAML:

```yaml
prose_formatting:
  paragraphs:
    rule: "No blank lines between paragraphs within a section"
    format: "Paragraphs flow together. Blank lines ONLY between section breaks (---)"
```

This documents the expectation for future sessions.

### Version Comparison

We produced multiple merged versions and compared them:

**Version A (my merge):** Included more Four lines, section headers, complete VTOL + waystation.

**Version B (other AI merge):** Tighter, fewer Four lines, better closing line.

**Final hybrid:** Used Version B's waystation as base, Version A's VTOL section, repositioned "voice of God's transportation system" line to after worship moment.

**Lesson:** Multiple drafts + comparison produces better output than single-pass generation.

I repeatedly added blank lines despite the existing chapters showing the correct format. Need to check existing files before generating new content.

### 2. Four's Line Discipline
Initial drafts had too many Four interjections. The rule: Four should punctuate, not narrate. 3-5 lines per chapter section, not 8.

### 3. Context Loading
I could have checked the voice cards earlier. The author had to point me to `VOICE_CARDS.md` during the session.

### 4. Scaffold Clarity
The beat sheet evolved during the session. Starting with a locked scaffold before any prose would have been more efficient.

## Thematic Notes

### The Central Question
"I know I'm not normal. I don't know what that makes me."

This is Standard's first explicit acknowledgment. She's not in denial — she's in uncertainty. The chapter positions her:
- Not human (doesn't eat, doesn't recharge, healed too fast)
- Not standard synthetic (no degradation, no recharge cycle)
- Something unnamed ("It makes you something they haven't named yet")

### The Spectrum
The author's key insight: In 2400 CE, there's a spectrum from baseline human to full synthetic. Standard doesn't know where she falls on it. Her uncertainty is philosophically coherent, not naive denial.

### The Gift Thread
The chapter advances the book's thesis through the worship scene:
- Standard doesn't ask to be worshipped
- She doesn't deserve it (she doesn't even know what she is)
- But Elena says: "Don't correct them."
- Standard plays a role she didn't choose, offering something she doesn't understand

This parallels the larger thesis: "What if they offer something we don't deserve?"
