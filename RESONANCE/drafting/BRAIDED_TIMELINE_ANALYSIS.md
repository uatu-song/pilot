## 2. TECHNIQUE: THE MONTAGE RULES

From the Montage Style Guide, four principles governed the intercutting:

### Rule 1: Maximum Two Timelines
Never intercut more than two threads in a single scene. The reader can track A and B. Adding C creates confusion.

**Implementation:** Scene breaks (---) handle thread switches. Within a scene, stay in one POV.

### Rule 2: Orient the Reader
After every scene break, establish location immediately. First line should ground the reader in space.

**Examples from Ch 32:**
- "They fight." (Throne room, immediate)
- "Standard is at his side in seconds." (Same room, time skip)
- "Four's voice is weaker now." (VTOL wreckage)
- "The first jump is clean." (Four's relay sequence)

### Rule 3: Pattern Creates Clarity
Consistent beat structure across threads. If Thread A gets setup-escalation-turn, Thread B should mirror it. The rhythm teaches the reader how to follow.

### Rule 4: Build Toward Convergence
Threads don't just alternate—they approach each other. The finale literally converges everyone in the throne room.

**Convergence sequence:**
1. Elena arrives (alone)
2. Standard + Four crash in (save Elena)
3. Hendricks returns (from the "dead")
4. Ash dies (all threads present)

## 3. METHODOLOGY: HOW IT WAS BUILT

### Phase 1: Scaffolding (Sessions 10-11)

Before any prose, detailed beat sheets were created:
- Scene-by-scene breakdowns
- Character positions tracked per beat
- Word count targets assigned per confrontation:
  - Major confrontation: 2,000+ words
  - Medium confrontation: 1,500+ words
  - High Noon (Hendricks returns): 500-800 words
  - "It's okay" (The Door): 2,000+ words

### Phase 2: Callback Tracking (Session 11)

A systematic tracker ensured all setups paid off:

| Setup | Location | Payoff | Location |
|-------|----------|--------|----------|
| "Does it still hurt the same?" | Ch 27 | Standard to Ash | Ch 32 |
| Ash cuts off Standard's arm | Ch 27 | Three arms progression | Ch 27, 31, 32 |
| Ceramic blade kills Morton | Book 1 | Same blade stabs Hendricks | Ch 31 |
| "I'll keep the engine warm" | Ch 30 | Four's sacrifice | Ch 33 |
| Revolver - 2 bullets remain | Ch 27 | Bullet 5 = Ash, 6 = Standard | Ch 32, 33 |
| Ch 1: Hendricks points at chest | Ch 1 | "It's okay" | Ch 33 |
| 16,749 souls in Black Box | Ch 20 | Same grace offered to Ash | Ch 33 |

### Phase 3: Negative Constraints (Session 12)

Explicit list of what NOT to do:

**Pacing:**
- Do NOT compress confrontations into single paragraphs
- Do NOT skip transitions (teleportation problem)
- Do NOT rush Four's death after 20+ chapters of buildup
- Do NOT jam all three Ash confrontations into one scene

**Character:**
- Do NOT make Ash stupid or cartoonish
- Do NOT let Hendricks explain himself (he acts, doesn't talk)
- Do NOT resolve Standard's consciousness question
- Do NOT have characters deliver thesis statements

**Action:**
- Do NOT use blow-by-blow choreography
- Do NOT allow action-movie survival without cost
- Do NOT write fights that don't reveal character

**Structure:**
- Do NOT explain the plan in dialogue
- Do NOT miss entire battlefronts
- Do NOT forget refugees picking up weapons (thesis goes both ways)

**Tone:**
- Do NOT use sentimentality (earn emotion through specificity)
- Do NOT use cynicism (the gift is real)
- Do NOT provide clean resolution (the door stays open, the cost is real)

### Phase 4: Character-Specific Drafting (Session 9 methodology)

The "Method Actor" approach from Session 9:
1. Generate character-specific drafts separately
2. Each voice stays distinct because it's written in isolation
3. Human editor merges, selecting best elements
4. Editorial pass balances comedy/drama ratios

**Applied to Act III:**
- Standard thread: Visceral, fragmentary, uncertain
- Hendricks thread: Clipped, action-focused, no introspection
- Elena thread: PTSD texture, competent but cracking
- Four thread: Dry humor thinning to sincerity

## 4. STRENGTHS OF THE APPROACH

### 1. Clarity Through Structure
The five-beat spine ensures every scene knows its purpose. Writers can ask: "Which beat is this? What must happen here?"

### 2. Earned Convergence
Threads build toward meeting rather than just alternating. The throne room convergence feels inevitable because we've watched everyone travel toward it.

### 3. Systematic Callbacks
The tracker prevents dropped threads. Every gun on the mantelpiece fires. "Does it still hurt the same?" lands because we tracked it.

### 4. Room to Breathe
Word count targets prevent the compression instinct. The brawl gets 2,000 words. "It's okay" gets its moment. Nothing is rushed.

### 5. Negative Constraints as Guardrails
Knowing what NOT to do is as valuable as knowing what to do. The constraints prevent common finale failures (thesis speeches, rushed confrontations, clean resolutions).

### 6. Dramatic Irony Engine
When reading Elena's thread, the reader knows Hendricks is falling. When reading Four's sacrifice, they know Standard is about to ask for the bullet. Holding all threads creates tension no single thread could.

---

## 5. WEAKNESSES AND CHALLENGES

### 1. Tracking Overhead
Five threads + 11 callbacks + 30 constraints = complex system. Easy to lose something without documentation.

### 2. Beat Rigidity
If a scene needs more room, the structure may resist. The Hendricks/Ash brawl could have been 3,000 words; the beat structure pushed for 2,000.

### 3. Macro War as Obligation
The New Geneva / Alpha Centauri thread can feel like checking a box rather than organic storytelling. It's necessary for the thesis (AI and humans fighting together) but harder to make visceral.

### 4. POV Discipline
Braided timelines tempt head-hopping. Session 12 noted friction about staying in single POV during chaotic scenes.

### 5. Transition Labor
Every thread switch needs a clean hand-off. That's 6+ transitions per chapter. Each one is a potential stumble point.

---

## 6. THE KEY INSIGHT

From ACT_III_MAPS.md, Section 4:

> "What's Happening Simultaneously"

The braided approach isn't just structure—it's **dramatic irony as architecture**.

The reader holds more information than any single character. When Elena confronts Ash, we know Hendricks is climbing back. When Four burns out, we know Standard is about to need her. The tension comes not from uncertainty about what happens next, but from knowing what's coming and watching characters walk toward it.

This is why the beat structure matters: it ensures all threads advance together, so the dramatic irony compounds rather than dissipates.

## 7. REPLICATION CHECKLIST

For future projects using this technique:

### Pre-Writing
- [ ] Identify all parallel threads (recommend 3-5 max)
- [ ] Define character arc for each thread (START → SHIFT → END)
- [ ] Create beat structure (5-7 beats typically)
- [ ] Assign beats to chapters
- [ ] Track all callbacks in a table
- [ ] Write negative constraints (what NOT to do)
- [ ] Assign word count targets per major scene

### During Writing
- [ ] Scaffold each chapter before prose
- [ ] Write character threads separately if possible
- [ ] Check first line after every scene break (orientation)
- [ ] Limit to 2 threads intercut per scene
- [ ] Verify callbacks are landing per tracker

### Post-Writing
- [ ] Read each thread in isolation (does it work alone?)
- [ ] Read all threads together (does convergence feel earned?)
- [ ] Check all callbacks against tracker
- [ ] Verify no constraint violations
